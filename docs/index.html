The forecast-driven policy re-forecasts demand every 7 days and re-sizes its reorder point to match — the static policy is set once from 180 days of history and never updates. As real demand grows past that stale average, the static policy quietly under-stocks. That's the whole story: not "AI wins outright," but a genuine service-vs-cost tradeoff, caused by one policy tracking a trend the other can't see.

Why Holt-Winters won

Four forecasters, backtested with 6-fold walk-forward validation (never just a single train/test split). Naive and gradient boosting are legitimate contenders; Holt-Winters wins because the demand process has the exact trend + weekly seasonality shape it's built to model directly.

Phase 3 — routing the network

The twin grows into a network: two regional warehouses, four retail stores, and a squeeze — southern demand outgrows the South warehouse's fixed inbound capacity over the year. Same demand, same replenishment, same information; the only difference is how warehouse stock gets routed. A min-cost transportation LP re-routes ~8,000 units/year of northern slack southward and its plan feeds back into the twin's state — closing the forecast → optimize → simulate loop.
